See Jane Garden

Ok, so my name isn't Jane but I am learning (again) to garden. Container Garden, that is. Why container gardening, you ask? I decided that, much like the George Straight song "Here for a Good Time", I want to DO rather than just dream about what I'd like to do. So what does that have to do with container gardening? Who dreams of container gardening? One word: Hawaii!!!


Huh? Hawaii? Yep. So here's the thing... I want to live on an island. It doesn't have to be Hawaii, there are several that would work. But island life isn't all "island time" and beaches. Island life brings the added challenge of expensive goods due to limited natural resources and high freight costs for anything that is shipped in.


One way that islanders can beat the high cost of living is by growing their own produce. Not surprisingly, however, land is at a premium so vertical and container gardening is the way to go. Enter PHASE 1 in preparing to live the dream: learning to container garden.


My Progress

Where I started...

That's right, I could kill anything through neglect. (I'm fairly certain that my kids only lived because they were noisy when they were hungry.) BUT, I grew gardens full of vegetables and flowers from seed when I was a kid so I knew I could do it again.


Year One


The first year actually somewhat by coincidence when I found some small wooden boxes at the Habitat Restore. The manager commented that his wife wanted to use them as garden boxes and he would give me a deal if he didn't have to take them ALL home. Well, far be it from me to pass up a deal! So I took the boxes, went to the nursery to buy plants, and I was hooked.


I went into "research mode", learning which plants would grow best in containers, the optimal soil mixture, and planting timelines. By the end of May, I had filled our patio with pots and convinced my husband to build a 4 foot by 8 foot raised bed. I had a lot of failures, from buying cheap potting soil that set up to a concrete-like state to not providing adequate drainage resulting in root rot. But I had some success too.

For a while that first summer, we had more squash and zucchini than we could eat until the garden succumbed to neglect while I was away on a business trip.


Year Two and Beyond


The second year was a furthering of the experiment and learning process. I went into the season with a full plan and a bit of confidence. What I didn't take into consideration; however, is that with this soil having been planted in before, there would be pests introduced/established that I hadn't had to deal with the first year. But every failure is a great learning opportunity and here are a few things I learned:

  • Those things that look like stink bugs are actually squash bugs. KILL THEM!
  • Move the tomato plants each year because hornworms are disgusting creatures.
  • Business trips are BAAADDDD for my garden. Alas, the bugs demolished the whole thing in one week of my absence.

This coming summer will be year three. I'll apply the knowledge I've gained so far and see what new challenges crop up.
